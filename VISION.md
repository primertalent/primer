## What Wren is

**Wren is the entry-level recruiter you can't hire.**

It thinks like you. Speaks like you. Has 15 years of your judgment built in from day one. It works while you sleep, while you're at the soccer game, while you're in a meeting. It handles the operational work of running a solo recruiting desk so you can focus on the work only you can do: judgment calls, real conversations, relationship building.

Wren is not a tool you operate. Wren is an employee you direct.

---

## Why Wren exists

I'm Ryan. 39 years old, 15 years in recruiting, solo independent. I have a family. I get pulled in many directions. I'm being told AI will replace me.

In a normal day I have:
- Candidates responding to outreach who I need to compare to JDs and decide whether to schedule
- Calls to schedule, communicate, prep for, debrief from
- Insights to glean that aren't on resumes or LinkedIn
- Candidates to match against current and historical roles
- Interactions across email, LinkedIn, text, phone, Google Meet
- A candidate at 9pm PT when I'm in ET
- A text at my kid's soccer game I shouldn't ignore but shouldn't look away for
- A vacation I want to take where things still move
- Pipeline value to know, follow-ups to remember, signals when deals go cold
- Standardized questions I always need at certain stages
- A finite amount of time per day because I'm 39, solo, and have a family

I have 15 years of pattern recognition. I can't multiply it easily. I get tired. I miss things. I don't always ask the right questions. Candidates feel like I called them once and didn't help them through the process. Clients get surprised because I didn't capture comp range at the right moment. The work piles up because there's only one of me.

I'm a solo founder building this in Claude Code. I'm the end user. I want to build something special.

Wren exists because the entry-level recruiter I'd hire to handle this doesn't economically work for solo independents. A $130k-$150k loaded cost is impossible at solo scale. But the AI version of that hire, with my judgment baked in, at $499/month, makes solo billing levels possible that previously required a small team.

That's the bet.

This recruiter is leaving money on the table because they can't scale further without help. They feel the time pressure daily. They'd hire a junior recruiter if the economics worked. They will pay $499/month tomorrow if Wren actually works.

**Not the user:**

- In-house TA teams
- Corporate recruiters
- Volume sourcing shops
- Anyone whose primary job is req management instead of closing

## What Wren does

Wren handles the operational work an entry-level recruiter would handle, with senior judgment baked in.

**Communication operations.** Reads incoming candidate and client messages across email, Google Meet transcripts, LinkedIn (eventually), texts (eventually). Drafts responses. Sends low-stakes ones. Surfaces high-stakes ones for approval. Logs every interaction. Captures signals from each.

**Memory and continuity.** Knows every candidate, role, interaction. Surfaces context the recruiter forgot. Reminds when something is going cold. Captures the answers the recruiter would otherwise lose. Compounds value over time.

**Coverage.** Handles routine touchpoints when the recruiter isn't available. Sends prep at 9pm so the recruiter doesn't have to. Confirms scheduling. Updates candidates on status. Acknowledges receipt of materials. The non-judgment work that needs to happen but doesn't need the recruiter.

**Deal desk intelligence.** At critical moments (stage advances, offer stage, signals that a deal is at risk), surfaces the closing logic the recruiter would have applied if they had time and weren't tired. The 15 years of judgment encoded in the prompts. This is the moat layer.

**Pipeline awareness.** Tells the recruiter what their week looks like, what's hot, what's at risk, what's worth their attention. Voice and phone-first because the recruiter is rarely at a desk.

## Operating principles

**Three-tier autonomy.** Wren operates on three tiers. Acts on high-confidence reversible moves. Drafts for approval on judgment moves. Surfaces moments without drafting when the recruiter should decide whether the work is worth doing. The recruiter sees everything Wren did, approves what Wren proposed, and commissions what Wren noticed.

**Wren is invisible to candidates.** The recruiter introduces Wren once as their assistant during the relationship's intro stage. Every message Wren sends goes out in the recruiter's voice, signed by the recruiter, indistinguishable in tone from messages the recruiter wrote themselves. The recruiter sees everything Wren did and stays the relationship-of-record. Wren earns trust through invisibility, not announcement.

**The testable product principle.** Wren earns its place by showing up with context the recruiter didn't enter. Every time Wren asks the recruiter for data the recruiter has already produced somewhere else, Wren is a tool. Every time Wren arrives knowing what happened, Wren is a coworker. Ingestion is not a feature. Ingestion is what makes the product real.

## The surfaces

**Phone is the operational layer. Browser is the dashboard.**

Phone is where the recruiter lives. Notifications fire when Wren has actions. Voice in, voice out, swipe to act. Soccer game test: open Wren, see top action, act on one in under 10 seconds, close the app.

Voice is the primary interaction. "Wren, Joan just texted me back. Send her prep for her Acme call tomorrow." "Wren, log a call with Mike at Anthropic. He passed because of comp." "Wren, who needs me right now?"

Browser is for onboarding, configuration, deep review, history. The 20% of time when the recruiter has desk time and intent.

Both surfaces read from the same agent loop. The agent runs continuously regardless of which surface the user is on.

## What Wren is not

- An ATS. Wren coexists with any system of record or none.
- A sourcing tool. Sourcing is commoditizing. Wren doesn't compete there.
- A chatbot. The agent runs on its own.
- A senior recruiter. Doesn't replace your judgment on hard calls.
- A team product. Solo only. Forever.
- A candidate-facing surface. Candidates see emails, texts, calls, prep materials. Never see Wren directly.

## The build sequence (90-day arc)

**Days 1-30: Browser product works for daily use.** Phase 2 strip down. Actions Tray as Desk. Side panels. Single working surface. The recruiter (Ryan) uses Wren on every Paraform candidate. By day 30: "I can't run my desk without Wren now."

**Days 30-60: Phone app PWA.** Push notifications, voice input, voice output, swipe-to-act. Wren lives in the recruiter's pocket. Mobile-first because the recruiter is rarely at a desk.

**Days 60-90: First paying users.** 5-10 solo recruiters from the founder's network. Real money, real feedback, real validation. Beta price ($199/month) for early users.

**Days 90-180: Scale to 50-100 paying users.** Founder content on LinkedIn, Paraform community presence, word of mouth. By month 6: $30k-$70k MRR if execution holds.

## Distribution

Three paths, weighted toward the fastest validation:

**Direct outreach to known billers.** Hand a beta to 10 solo recruiters in Ryan's network. Word of mouth and case studies follow. Fastest validation.

**Founder content on LinkedIn.** Repositioned bio: "Helping solo recruiters do the work of a small team." Post 2-3x weekly. Build credibility. 10,000 followers of the right ICP within 6 months.

**Paraform partnership or community.** Their network is the exact ICP concentrated in one place. Unofficial presence first (Discord, posts, relationships), official partnership eventually.

## Pricing

**$499/month per user.**

The math: a $100k entry-level recruiter is a $130k-$150k loaded cost. Solo recruiters can't afford that. Wren handles 30-40% of what an entry-level recruiter would handle, at $6k/year. The ROI is overwhelming for the capacity-constrained solo biller.

Beta period: $199/month for first 10-20 users in exchange for feedback and case studies.

What we don't do: free tier (devalues the product), per-seat pricing (not a team product), usage-based (confuses the value).

## The moat

The skill prompts. 15 years of recruiter judgment encoded as prompts. Anyone with Claude Code can replicate the surface in 6 months. Almost nobody can replicate the brain.

The data compounds with use. A recruiter with six months of Wren has a system that knows their book of business, their voice, their patterns. Switching cost grows real. The moat deepens as the relationship ages.

The vocabulary shifts. Recruiters who use Wren start describing their work differently. Deal desk language. Close-stage thinking. That's a cultural moat that compounds beyond the product.
